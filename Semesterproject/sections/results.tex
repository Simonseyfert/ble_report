\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The system was successfully implemented and validated across all major functional blocks:
hardware-driven neural signal acquisition across a configurable selection of 1--16 channels,
charge-balanced biphasic electrical stimulation, and bidirectional BLE command and data
transfer. Key quantitative results are summarised here; detailed measurements and figures
are presented in the subsections that follow.

BLE throughput with real SPI data from the Intan RHS2116 reached up to
approximately 540\,kbps in the default 16-channel configuration at 2\,kHz per channel,
confirming that the hardware-driven pipeline sustains the required data rate under
continuous operation; no packet loss was observed under stable operating conditions.
The stimulation pipeline was validated by oscilloscope
measurement of the biphasic waveform at the Intan RHS2116 output, confirming correct pulse
shape, timing, and charge balance across a range of configured parameters
(Section~\ref{sec:results_stim}). Multi-channel neural signal recording was confirmed by
live acquisition from the RHS2116, with all configured channels rendered in real time in
the GUI (Section~\ref{sec:results_recording}).

Power consumption of the nRF52832 peripheral module was measured at 18.6\,mW under
continuous 16-channel recording at 2\,kHz per channel at the measured supply voltage
of 3.020\,V (equivalent to 20.4\,mW at a nominal 3.3\,V). This compares favourably
with the 29.4\,mW at 3.3\,V reported by the prior single-channel
implementation~\cite{prior_impl}: at a common reference voltage of 3.3\,V, the present
work achieves a 31\,\% reduction in module power while simultaneously increasing the
channel count 16-fold. Two architectural improvements account for this: the PPI/DMA
acquisition pipeline allows the CPU to remain in low-power sleep between BLE events
rather than spinning in a polling loop, and removal of the UART peripheral eliminates
a further source of continuous power draw. Combined power consumption of the nRF52832 and Intan RHS2116 was measured at
61.3\,mW at 3.6\,V and 48.6\,mW at 3.0\,V in the same 16-channel, 2\,kHz per
channel configuration. Full power measurements across all channel and sampling
rate configurations at both supply voltages are presented in
Section~\ref{sec:results_power} and Appendix~\ref{app:power_data}.

\subsection{Neural Signal Recording}
\label{sec:results_recording}

The end-to-end recording pipeline was validated by injecting a 300\,Hz, 1\,mV
sinusoidal signal from a Keysight 33500B Waveform
Generator~\cite{keysight_33500b} into the Intan RHS2116
input via the carrier board connector. This frequency corresponds to the upper
limit of the LFP band targeted by the system~\cite{reimann2013biophysically}
and was chosen specifically to demonstrate faithful reconstruction of the
highest-frequency LFP components of interest. The 1\,mV amplitude is well
within the
$\pm5$\,mV input range of the amplifier. A range of channel-count and sampling-rate combinations was characterised;
two representative configurations are presented here:
16 channels at 2\,kHz per channel (the default operating point) and 8 channels
at 4\,kHz per channel (higher temporal resolution at reduced channel count).

Figures~\ref{fig:rec_16ch} and~\ref{fig:rec_8ch} show representative recorded
traces for both configurations. In both cases the 300\,Hz waveform is faithfully
reconstructed with correct frequency and amplitude, and no dropped samples are
visible in either trace. As expected, the 4\,kHz configuration produces a
slightly smoother reconstruction of the sinusoidal waveform due to the higher
number of samples per period (13.3 samples/period at 4\,kHz vs.\ 6.7 at 2\,kHz).

\subsection{Power Consumption}
\label{sec:results_power}

Combined (nRF52832\,+\,Intan RHS2116) power consumption was measured at both
3.0\,V and 3.6\,V supply; 3.6\,V corresponds to the output voltage of the
wireless power transfer system, while 3.0\,V serves as a lower-voltage
reference, representative of the nRF52832's supply voltage when powered via USB. Full measurement tables for both voltages are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:power_data}. The nRF52832-only reference value was measured
at the actual bench supply of 3.020\,V (equivalent to 20.4\,mW at 3.3\,V).

\subsubsection*{Power vs.\ Channel Count at Fixed 2\,kHz per Channel}

Figure~\ref{fig:power_2khz} shows combined system power consumption as a function
of the number of active recording channels, all operated at 2\,kHz per channel.
Power increases approximately linearly from 43.7\,mW (1 channel) to 61.3\,mW
(16 channels). The 1-channel case falls below the linear extrapolation of the
2--16-channel trend: in single-channel mode the SPI bus runs at only 2\,kHz
(one \texttt{CONVERT($k$)} transaction per sample), whereas in multi-channel mode
\texttt{CONVERT(63)} runs at $2\,\text{kHz} \times 16 = 32$\,kHz regardless of
how many channels are selected. The lower SPI activity reduces power draw in both
the nRF52832 (fewer DMA transactions) and the Intan RHS2116 (fewer ADC conversion
cycles). The total SPI transaction rate is identical across the 2--16-channel
configurations (32\,kHz), since \texttt{CONVERT(63)} cycles through all 16 channels
regardless of how many are selected; only the selected samples are forwarded over BLE,
so actual BLE throughput varies from 4\,000 samples/s (2 channels) to 32\,000 samples/s
(16 channels).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/power_consumption_at_2kHz_per_channel.png}
    \caption{Combined nRF52832 and Intan RHS2116 power consumption at 3.6\,V as a
    function of the number of active recording channels, all configured at
    2\,kHz per channel. Power increases approximately linearly with channel count
    (43.7\,mW for 1 channel to 61.3\,mW for 16 channels). The 1-channel case

\subsubsection*{Maximum Achievable Rate and Power vs.\ Channel Count}

Figure~\ref{fig:power_maxrate} shows the maximum achievable per-channel sampling
rate and the corresponding combined power consumption as a function of channel
count. For 1 channel and 4--16 channels the system is BLE-limited: the total useful
throughput is capped at 32\,000 samples/s, and for the multi-channel configurations
the per-channel rate decreases inversely with channel count. For 2 and 3 channels the system is instead SPI-limited: the SPI
bus reaches its empirical maximum of 128\,kHz, yielding 8\,kHz per channel, but
the resulting BLE throughput (16\,000 and 24\,000 samples/s respectively) is
below the 32\,000\,samples/s BLE ceiling. The reduced BLE activity produces a
visible dip in power for these two configurations.

The 1-channel configuration at its maximum rate of 32\,kHz per channel has the
same SPI rate (32\,kHz) and BLE throughput (32\,000 samples/s) as the
16-channel, 2\,kHz configuration, and would therefore be expected to show
comparable power. The measured value is consistently higher, and the difference can be attributed to
the Intan RHS2116 command mode: comparing configurations with identical SPI
transaction rates and BLE throughput isolates the command sequence as the sole
varying factor. At 16\,kHz SPI (1-channel 16\,kHz vs.\ 16-channel 1\,kHz per channel),
\texttt{CONVERT($k$)} draws approximately 7--8\,\% more power than
\texttt{CONVERT(63)} across both supply voltages. At 32\,kHz SPI (1-channel 32\,kHz
vs.\ 16-channel 2\,kHz per channel) the excess is approximately 12--14\,\%, consistent
across 3.0\,V and 3.6\,V and reproduced in the second independent 3.6\,V measurement
run. The mechanism is not documented in the RHS2116 datasheet and cannot be confirmed
from available documentation; the excess likely reflects an internal difference in
how the chip handles the two command types, but this remains speculative. This finding has a counterintuitive implication for system design. A
\texttt{CONVERT($k$)}-based architecture for 2--3-channel configurations would
avoid the fixed $16\times$ SPI overhead: two channels at 2\,kHz would require only
4\,kHz total SPI transactions instead of 32\,kHz, raising the per-channel rate
ceiling and intuitively suggesting a power saving. The present data, however, show
that each \texttt{CONVERT($k$)} transaction is 7--14\,\% more expensive than a
\texttt{CONVERT(63)} transaction at the same total SPI rate. This is itself the
counterintuitive part: at a fixed SPI rate, the two command types might naively be
expected to cost about the same, or if anything \texttt{CONVERT(63)} slightly more
due to the internal multiplexing logic it requires (though this overhead is likely
negligible) --- not the reverse. Since a \texttt{CONVERT($k$)}-based architecture
would nonetheless send fewer total transactions for the same number of selected
channels, whether this per-transaction overhead would be fully or partially offset by the
reduction in transaction count is not determinable from the available data; a direct
measurement of a \texttt{CONVERT($k$)}-based multi-channel implementation would be
required.

\subsubsection*{Power Consumption Time Course}

Figure~\ref{fig:power_timecourse} shows the power consumption over time for the
1-channel, 2\,kHz per channel configuration at 3.6\,V. The full 2.866\,s trace
(top) reveals eight periodic spikes with an inter-spike interval of approximately
400\,ms. This matches the expected DMA buffer-swap period exactly: in
single-channel mode at 2\,kHz, one SPI transaction occurs every 500\,\textmu{}s,
so the Timer2 threshold of 819 transactions fires after $819 \times 500\,\mu\text{s}
= 409.5\,\text{ms}$. Each spike peaks at approximately 27\,mA (97\,mW at 3.6\,V),
reflecting the combined cost of copying the inactive DMA buffer into the ring buffer
and assembling and transmitting the BLE packets.

The 40\,ms zoomed window (bottom) resolves the BLE connection-event structure.
Five peaks appear at 10\,ms intervals, empirically confirming that connection
events occur at 10\,ms --- consistent with the connection interval reported by
the SoftDevice connection-established callback during development. Each event
shows a double-peak structure: a smaller peak ($\approx 15$\,mA, 54\,mW)
followed approximately 1.5\,ms later by a larger peak ($\approx 19$\,mA, 68\,mW),
consistent with the sequential radio interrupts within a single connection event
described in Appendix~\ref{app:softdevice_timing}.

The large periodic spikes arise from the CPU work at each DMA buffer swap: copying
819 sample-entries from the inactive DMA buffer into the ring buffer and preparing
BLE packets (see Section~\ref{sec:discussion_ringbuffer} for a discussion of
alternative architectures).

\subsubsection*{Effect of Supply Voltage: 3.0\,V vs 3.6\,V}

Power consumption at 3.0\,V is uniformly lower than at 3.6\,V across all
configurations, with a measured ratio $P(3.0\,\text{V})\,/\,P(3.6\,\text{V})
= 0.765 \pm 0.006$ (mean\,$\pm$\,std). The spread is below $1\,\%$,
confirming that the scaling is essentially configuration-independent.
The ratio falls between the predictions of a purely LDO-dominated model
($R = 0.833$, $P \propto V$; excess supply voltage dissipated as heat in a
linear regulator) and a purely dynamic-switching-dominated model
($R = 0.694$, $P \propto V^2$; CMOS switching losses scale as $CV^2\!f$),
consistent with an effective power-law exponent $\alpha \approx 1.47$,
indicating roughly equal contributions from both mechanisms. The full
derivation and comparison figure are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:power_voltage}.

\subsubsection*{Measurement Repeatability}

The 3.6\,V measurements were repeated in a second independent session
(Table~\ref{tab:power_repeat} in Appendix~\ref{app:power_data}).
At 2\,kHz per channel the two runs agreed to within 3.8\,\% on average
(std 1.0\,\%) across all channel counts; at maximum per-channel rate
the agreement was 5.9\,\% (std 3.2\,\%). Both runs reproduce the same trends:
approximately linear growth with channel count at 2\,kHz per channel, and the
BLE-limited plateau with the characteristic power dip for 2--3 channels at
maximum rate. The residual spread is consistent with a small difference in
effective supply voltage between sessions: as the voltage scaling analysis in
Appendix~\ref{app:power_voltage} shows, even a modest supply offset produces a
proportional change in absolute power across the board, while leaving trends
unaffected. Comparison
plots for the two runs are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:power_repeat}.

\subsection{Electromagnetic Interference in the WPT Field}
\label{sec:results_noise}

Miniaturising the peripheral module served two purposes. First, implantable
neural devices need to be compact, particularly for small-animal applications
such as the rat spinal cord targeted here, where volume and weight constraints
are far tighter than in larger animals or humans. Second, and equally important, is
compatibility with wireless power delivery: beyond simply fitting within the
receiver coil footprint, long external wires and loops were found to pick up
severe interference from the WPT field --- as shown below, fully saturating the
amplifier input --- so collocating the chips on a single compact module is
central to WPT compatibility, not just to reducing footprint. Using the WPT system developed in
parallel within the lab (developed by Marta Vasconcelos), the complete peripheral --- nRF52832 and
Intan RHS2116 --- was operated with no wired power supply. This represents the
most significant system-level result of this work: the full wireless architecture
(wireless power \emph{and} wireless data) was validated end-to-end for the first
time. The measurements below first confirm successful operation, then quantify the
electromagnetic interference introduced by the transmitter coil field.

\subsubsection*{Basic Wireless Power Operation}

With the peripheral powered via the WPT system and the Intan RHS2116 located
outside the transmitter coil field, the 300\,Hz sinusoidal test signal was
successfully acquired and transmitted over BLE, confirming end-to-end operation
under wireless power. Following a momentary power interruption and automatic
reconnection, BLE throughput recovered to its maximum value within approximately
2--3\,s; data acquired during this ramp-up period are saved but should be
discarded in post-processing.

\subsubsection*{Interference with Carrier Board in the WPT Field}

Placing the Intan RHS2116 on its carrier board inside the transmitter coil field
rendered the 300\,Hz sinusoidal test input unrecoverable. Two coupled mechanisms
contribute, both bench-specific and absent in a final implanted system:
(1)~the external cables from the function generator used to inject the test signal
act as antennas, and (2)~the carrier board's own traces and wiring loops form
inductive pickup paths in the alternating magnetic field of the WPT transmitter,
even without external cables attached.

To characterise the interference quantitatively without the confound of the input
signal, channel~7 was shorted to GND on the carrier board (nearest recording channel
to the GND supply pin; see Figure~\ref{fig:connector_pinout}). The results at three
distances are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:noise_shorted}.

At 30\,cm from the coil edge the interference is modest: peak-to-peak excursions
are mostly within $\pm10$\,\textmu{}V (20\,\textmu{}V$_\text{pp}$), with occasional
peaks reaching $\pm15$\,\textmu{}V (30\,\textmu{}V$_\text{pp}$). This is
approximately twice the amplifier's intrinsic noise floor of
$\approx15.8$\,\textmu{}V$_\text{pp}$ (Section~\ref{sec:hardware_intan}), indicating
that environmental sources already contribute measurably at this distance; even a
smartphone in the vicinity was observed to introduce additional interference.
At 4\,cm the interference increases substantially, with excursions reaching
$+50$ to $+60$\,\textmu{}V and down to $-90$\,\textmu{}V. Inside the coil the
signal saturates the amplifier input entirely, spanning from $-6000$ to
$+4000$\,\textmu{}V.

\subsubsection*{Direct On-Chip Measurement}

Shorting channel~1 directly on the Intan RHS2116 die, bypassing the carrier board
and its wiring, reduced the interference to $\pm20$--$30$\,\textmu{}V
(Figure~\ref{fig:shorted_on_intan}) --- a reduction of approximately 200-fold
relative to the $-6000$ to $+4000$\,\textmu{}V saturation observed with the
carrier board inside the coil. This two-orders-of-magnitude improvement confirms
that the carrier board wiring --- rather than the chip itself or the WPT field
acting directly on the silicon --- is the dominant pickup path. The residual
$\pm20$--$30$\,\textmu{}V reflects hand-soldered connections still forming small
pickup loops; microfabricated traces on a co-integrated PCB would probably reduce these
further.

\subsubsection*{Flex PCB}

The prototype flex PCB co-integrating the nRF52832 and Intan RHS2116
(Figure~\ref{fig:miniaturization}d) was intended to provide a defined test signal
inside the WPT field via its on-board square-wave oscillator, avoiding all external
signal wiring. However, the oscillator did not produce output even outside the WPT
field, indicating a hardware fault unrelated to interference. Wireless power
transfer characterisation with this module could therefore not be performed.
The result nonetheless illustrates the motivation for the co-integrated form
factor: eliminating external wiring is the key step toward making the system
compatible with the WPT field.
