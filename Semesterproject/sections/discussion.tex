\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Electromagnetic Interference in the WPT Field}

At 30\,cm from the transmitter coil, the recorded noise on a GND-shorted channel
is typically within $\pm10$\,\textmu{}V (20\,\textmu{}V$_\text{pp}$), only
marginally above the amplifier's intrinsic noise floor of
$\approx15.8$\,\textmu{}V$_\text{pp}$. At this distance the WPT field contributes
minimally and the system would likely support useful neural recordings. However,
interference grows rapidly with proximity, and inside the coil the amplifier input
saturates entirely. The direct on-chip measurement --- shorting a channel with no
carrier board wiring --- reduced the interference to $\pm20$--$30$\,\textmu{}V
even inside the coil, a three- to four-fold improvement. This confirms that the
carrier board wiring, rather than the WPT field acting directly on the silicon, is
the dominant pickup path: long external wires form inductive loops and act as
antennas in the alternating magnetic field.

The implication is that the interference problem is primarily a packaging problem,
not an intrinsic incompatibility between BLE-based neural recording and WPT.
A co-integrated PCB with short, shielded traces connecting the nRF52832 and Intan
RHS2116 would dramatically reduce pickup loop area and antenna length. The
prototype flex PCB (Figure~\ref{fig:miniaturization}d) represents exactly this
direction; its oscillator circuit was non-functional in the current revision, but
the hardware fault is unrelated to the interference mechanism and does not
undermine this conclusion.

\subsection{Power Consumption and Architectural Improvements}

The present system achieves a 31\,\% reduction in nRF52832 module power compared
to the prior single-channel CPU-polling implementation~\cite{prior_impl}, while
simultaneously increasing the channel count 16-fold. Two architectural changes
account for this. First, replacing the CPU polling loop with a PPI/EasyDMA
acquisition pipeline allows the processor to remain in low-power sleep between
BLE connection events rather than spinning continuously; the power timecourse data
confirm this, showing a low baseline current punctuated only by the periodic
connection event peaks. Second, removal of the UART peripheral --- which was used
in the prior implementation to forward data to the computer --- eliminates a
further source of continuous power draw.

\subsection{Ring Buffer Architecture}
\label{sec:discussion_ringbuffer}

The periodic power spikes visible in the timecourse data arise from CPU activity
at each DMA buffer swap: copying 819 samples from the inactive DMA buffer into the
ring buffer and assembling BLE packets. An alternative architecture could send data
directly from the inactive DMA buffer, potentially reducing these spikes. However,
this carries two costs. First, the ring buffer stores only the selected channels at
2\,bytes per sample, whereas the DMA buffers hold all 16 channels at 4\,bytes
each; in a 2-channel recording the ring buffer is 16$\times$ more memory-efficient
per acquisition cycle, allowing proportionally more data to be buffered in the
available RAM. Second, removing the ring buffer reintroduces a synchronisation
problem between the SPI EasyDMA write path and the BLE read path, requiring
careful atomic coordination that the current double-buffer architecture cleanly
avoids. This optimisation is left for future work if power becomes the primary
constraint.

\subsection{BLE Throughput}

BLE throughput of up to approximately 540\,kbps with real SPI data from the Intan
RHS2116 confirms that the hardware-driven pipeline successfully removes the CPU
bottleneck that limited the prior implementation. In the prior work, 600\,kbps was
reported, but this figure was achieved with synthetic data only; with real SPI
acquisition running concurrently, the CPU had insufficient time between SPI
transactions to sustain reliable BLE transmission, limiting the system to a single
channel at 2\,kHz. The present architecture decouples acquisition from the CPU
almost entirely, sustaining the required throughput across all tested channel and
sampling rate configurations with no packet loss.

\subsection{Limitations}

The carrier board used throughout this work is not representative of a final
implantable form factor. All measurements of interference and power consumption
reflect a bench-top assembly with long external wires, which as discussed above
is the dominant source of WPT-induced noise. Power measurements were made at room
temperature; thermal effects under implanted conditions have not been characterised.
The prototype flex PCB co-integrating the nRF52832 and Intan RHS2116 was
non-functional in its current revision, and neural recording with the most compact
hardware configuration could not be demonstrated. All signal recording was
validated using a function generator input rather than real neural signals from
electrodes.

\subsection{Future Directions}

The immediate next step is PCB co-integration of the nRF52832 and Intan RHS2116
on a single substrate with short, shielded SPI traces, eliminating the carrier
board wiring that currently dominates WPT interference. Beyond this, the PCB
should be realised as a microfabricated device on a thin-film substrate compatible
with implantable electrode arrays, combining the recording and stimulation
electronics with the electrode interface in a compact, biocompatible form factor.
Validation should then proceed first in vitro --- confirming signal integrity and
noise performance in a controlled environment without biological variability ---
before advancing to in vivo implantation studies to assess chronic performance
and tissue compatibility.
