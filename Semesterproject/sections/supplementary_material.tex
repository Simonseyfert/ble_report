\subsection{SoftDevice Preemption Timing Derivation}
\label{app:softdevice_timing}

The Nordic S132 SoftDevice preempts the application at every BLE connection 
event to handle radio and stack operations. The timing parameters for a 
peripheral connection event are given in Table~36 of the S132 SoftDevice
Specification v7.2.0~\cite{nordic_s132_sds_v72}, reproduced in 
Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing} below. The system does not employ encryption, 
PA/LNA, or the Radio Timeslot API, so typical values apply.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

(reproduced from Nordic S132 SDS v7.2.0, Table~36~\cite{nordic_s132_sds_v72}).}
\label{tab:softdevice_timing}
\begin{tabular}{llccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Min} & 
\textbf{Typical} & \textbf{Max} \\
\midrule
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioPrepare}}$    & Preparing radio for connection event        & ---          & 51~\textmu s      & 65~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioStart}}$      & Starting the connection event               & ---          & 18~\textmu s      & 24~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}}$ & Processing after sending/receiving a packet & ---          & 60~\textmu s      & 67~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{PostProcessing}}$  & Processing at end of connection event       & ---          & 90~\textmu s      & 250~\textmu s \\
$t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$                       & Distance between interrupts                 & 183~\textmu s& $>$190~\textmu s  & ---           \\

\subsection*{Single-Packet Baseline}

The datasheet gives the following formula for one packet sent and received 
per connection event:

For compactness, let $t_\text{RP}$, $t_\text{RS}$, $t_\text{RProc}$, 
$t_\text{PP}$, and $t_4$ denote the timing parameters from 
Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing}. The total preemption time for one 
packet is then:

\begin{equation}
t_{\text{1 pkt}} = t_\text{RP} + t_\text{RS} + t_\text{RProc} + 
t_\text{PP} + 2 \cdot t_4 = 299~\mu\text{s (typical)}
\label{eq:single_packet}
\end{equation}

The factor of $2 \cdot t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ reflects two priority-level-4 interrupts 
fired at the end of the event for stack cleanup and application event delivery, 
both associated with packet reception processing.

\subsection*{Extension to Multiple Packets per Connection Event}

At a sampling rate of 32\,000 SPI transactions per second across 16 channels, 
and with 112 samples per BLE packet, the required packet rate is:

\begin{equation}
\frac{32{,}000}{112} \approx 285.7 \text{ packets/s}
\end{equation}

The firmware configures a connection interval range of 7.5--10\,ms; the
empirically negotiated value throughout all experiments in this work was 10\,ms
(confirmed by inspection of the power consumption time course,
Figure~\ref{fig:power_timecourse}). At 10\,ms:

\begin{equation}
285.7 \times 0.010 \approx 2.9 \text{ packets per connection event}
\end{equation}

For completeness, at the minimum configured interval of 7.5\,ms:

\begin{equation}
285.7 \times 0.0075 \approx 2.1 \text{ packets per connection event}
\end{equation}

The system therefore transmits 2--3 packets per connection event; since the
negotiated interval is 10\,ms, the 3-packet scenario is the relevant upper bound. Each 
additional packet beyond the first adds one further 
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}}$ interrupt (to handle the 
acknowledgement) and one $t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ interrupt (to deliver the 
\texttt{HVN\_TX\_COMPLETE} event to the application, which triggers 
transmission of the next packet):

\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\text{per extra packet}} = 
t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}} + t_{\text{ISR}(4)} = 
60 + 40 = 100~\text{\textmu s (typical)}
\end{equation}

The total preemption time for 2 and 3 packets per event is therefore:

\begin{align}
t_{\text{2 packets}} &= 299 + 100 = 399~\text{\textmu s} \\
t_{\text{3 packets}} &= 299 + 200 = 499~\text{\textmu s}
\end{align}

This gives a total SoftDevice CPU time of approximately 400--500\,\textmu{}s per
connection event, occurring every 10\,ms (empirically negotiated value; configured
range 7.5--10\,ms). These ISRs are not executed consecutively: they are separated
by gaps of at least $t_{n\text{ISR}(0)} = 183$\,\textmu{}s during which the CPU
is available to the application.

\subsection*{Meaning of $t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$ and Non-Consecutive Execution}

$t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$ is the minimum time between two consecutive priority-0 ISR
firings within a single connection event. The $\geq$183\,\textmu{}s gaps between
ISRs represent idle CPU time during which application interrupts can execute.
\texttt{timer2\_count\_handler} (priority~6) therefore does not wait for the
entire 700\,\textmu{}s of SoftDevice CPU time to elapse --- it runs as soon as
the currently executing SoftDevice ISR and any immediately following
$t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ complete. The 183\,\textmu{}s gap is also far larger than
the DMA buffer swap itself ($\approx 250$\,ns), guaranteeing the swap completes
before the next SoftDevice ISR fires.

\subsection*{Total SoftDevice CPU Time per Connection Event}

Using the maximum column values from Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing} (note:
$t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ has no published maximum; typical value of 40\,\textmu{}s used):
\begin{align}
t_{\text{1 pkt, max}} &= 65 + 24 + 67 + 250 + 2 \times 40 = 486~\text{\textmu{}s} \\
t_{\text{2 pkts, max}} &= 486 + (67 + 40) = 593~\text{\textmu{}s} \\
t_{\text{3 pkts, max}} &= 486 + 2 \times (67 + 40) = 700~\text{\textmu{}s}
\end{align}

These values represent the total SoftDevice CPU time per connection event, not a
single continuous block.

\subsection*{Worst-Case Handler Delay}

Since \texttt{timer2\_count\_handler} runs in the inter-ISR gaps, the relevant
bound is the maximum duration of any single blocking sequence. The longest
priority-0 ISR is $t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{PostProcessing}}$ at 250\,\textmu{}s
(max), immediately followed by one $t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ firing at 40\,\textmu{}s
(typical; no maximum published). The worst-case delay before the handler executes
is therefore:
\begin{equation}
t_{\text{delay,max}} = t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{PostProcessing,max}} + t_{\text{ISR}(4)}
= 250 + 40 = 290~\mu\text{s}
\end{equation}

Assigning \texttt{timer2\_count\_handler} to interrupt priority~2 or~3 (permitted
under S132 --- only priorities 0 and~1 are SoftDevice-reserved --- but not
implemented here) would prevent $t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ at priority~4 from preempting
the handler, reducing the bound to 250\,\textmu{}s. This is not necessary given
the existing margin.

\subsection*{Swap Window}

At the maximum SPI rate of 128\,kHz (8\,kHz per channel; applicable to any channel
count in multi-channel mode), the time available to complete the DMA buffer swap
before the active buffer overflows is:
\begin{equation}
t_{\text{swap window}} =
(\texttt{DMA\_BUF\_SAMPLES} - 819) \times \frac{1}{128\,\text{kHz}}
= 205 \times 7.8125~\mu\text{s} \approx 1.6~\text{ms}
\end{equation}
The worst-case handler delay of 290\,\textmu{}s is safely within the 1.6\,ms
window by a factor of more than five.
At the default configuration (16 channels, 2\,kHz per channel, 32\,kHz SPI), the
window expands to $205 \times 31.25~\mu\text{s} \approx 6.4\,\text{ms}$.

\subsection{Supply Voltage Scaling Analysis}
\label{app:power_voltage}

To quantify the voltage dependence of power consumption, all configurations were
measured at both 3.0\,V and 3.6\,V. Two limiting theoretical models predict
different scaling laws for the ratio $R = P(3.0\,\text{V})\,/\,P(3.6\,\text{V})$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LDO-dominated} ($P \propto V$): excess supply voltage is
    dissipated as heat in a linear regulator, giving
    \begin{equation}
        R_{\text{LDO}} = \frac{3.0}{3.6} \approx 0.833
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic switching-dominated} ($P \propto V^2$, from
    $P_{\text{dyn}} = CV^2f$): CMOS switching losses scale with the square of the
    supply voltage, giving
    \begin{equation}
        R_{\text{CV}^2\!f} = \left(\frac{3.0}{3.6}\right)^{\!2} \approx 0.694
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

The measured ratio across all operating configurations
(Figure~\ref{fig:power_voltage}) has mean $0.765 \pm 0.006$ (std), falling
between the two theoretical references and almost exactly at their midpoint
($0.764$). The low standard deviation ($<1\,\%$) confirms that the voltage
scaling is essentially configuration-independent. An effective exponent $\alpha$
defined by $(3.0/3.6)^\alpha = 0.765$ can be computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \frac{\ln(0.765)}{\ln(3.0/3.6)}
           = \frac{-0.268}{-0.182} \approx 1.47
\end{equation}
This intermediate value ($1 < \alpha < 2$) suggests approximately equal
contributions from linear (LDO-type) dissipation and quadratic (CMOS switching)
losses.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/3V_vs_3_6V.png}
    \caption{Power consumption at 3.6\,V vs 3.0\,V across all measured operating

    throughput; grey lines connect matched operating points.
    \textit{Bottom:} measured power ratio $P(3.0\,\text{V})\,/\,P(3.6\,\text{V})$

\subsection{Practical Tips}

This section collects practical setup and troubleshooting information
for working with the system, aimed at future users continuing development.

\subsubsection{Software Requirements: Running the System}
\label{app:software_run}

To run the GUI without modifying firmware, the only prerequisite is the
\textbf{J-Link Software and Documentation Pack} (V8.18 or
later),\footnote{\url{https://www.segger.com/downloads/jlink/}~--- select
\textit{J-Link Software and Documentation Pack}, V8.18.}
which provides the J-Link drivers required by the \texttt{pylink-square}
library for RTT communication with the central DK.

Pre-built executables for Windows, macOS, and Linux are available as
release artefacts on the project
repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Simonseyfert/ble_spi_intan}~---
access upon request; see Section~\ref{sec:gui}.}
and can be launched directly without a Python installation.
To run from source, Python~3.10 or later is required; install dependencies
with \texttt{pip install -r requirements.txt} and launch the main GUI script.

\paragraph{Quick Start.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Connect the central nRF52832 DK to the computer via USB and power it on.
    \item Power on the peripheral module (connected to the Intan RHS2116).
    \item Launch the GUI. Once the BLE connection is established LED2 on the
          DK lights up.
    \item Click \textbf{Start Recording}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Code Development}

The following lessons were learned during the development of this project and are
intended to help future developers avoid common pitfalls.

\paragraph{On the Use of AI Assistants.}
Do not expect to describe the desired functionality or overall architecture to a
large language model and have working embedded firmware in return --- in practice,
nothing will work. Embedded systems programming is fundamentally different from
writing Python scripts: correctness depends on hardware timing, peripheral register
sequences, interrupt priorities, and DMA alignment, none of which an AI can verify
without running the code. That said, AI can still be useful: keeping the
\texttt{main.c} files open in a tool such as Claude Code gives it access to the
current firmware state and allows it to assist with targeted questions. Treat its
output as a starting point to read critically, not as code to paste blindly --- the
closer the change is to the hardware, the more likely it is to contain subtle
errors. Always cross-check generated code against the relevant datasheet.

\paragraph{Step-by-Step Hardware Bring-Up.}
New hardware interactions should be developed incrementally, in the smallest
testable steps possible. For example, before implementing the full PPI/DMA
acquisition chain, first verify that the timer can toggle the CS line alone. Add
one PPI channel at a time, confirm each step with the oscilloscope before
proceeding, and only combine components once each is individually validated.
Attempting to implement the full architecture in one step --- especially anything
involving new PPI channels or switching between CPU and hardware control of
peripherals such as the CS line --- will almost always fail and is very difficult
to debug.

\paragraph{Use the Oscilloscope.}
Hardware signals should be verified directly with an oscilloscope, not inferred
from software behaviour. Checking the CS line, SCLK, and MISO/MOSI waveforms
at each bring-up step catches problems that would otherwise produce silent failures
or misleading data.

\paragraph{Example.}
When transitioning the CS line from CPU-driven control (via the standard SPI
library) to hardware-driven control via GPIOTE and PPI, the new code appeared to
work correctly on the first test. What was not immediately obvious was why: because
the nRF52832 DK is power-cycled during flashing but VDD on the pin headers remains
active (supplied by the DK's USB regulator), the Intan RHS2116 had never lost
power and still held its register configuration from the previous session.

In the new code, SPIM was initialised with \texttt{NRFX\_SPIM\_PIN\_NOT\_USED}
as the chip-select pin from the outset, leaving CS for GPIOTE to control.
The Intan initialisation sequence was still present and called at startup, but
because SPIM had no ss\_pin, CS was never asserted during those register writes:
the SPI transactions went out on MOSI but the Intan ignored every one of them.
The chip silently inherited the register state from the previous session, so
everything appeared to work.

The following morning, after the setup had been powered off overnight, the same
code produced no output at all. Reverting to the old firmware --- which did include
a working initialisation --- restored operation. Flashing the new code immediately
afterward also worked, because the old firmware had just reinitialised the chip.
This alternating pattern (old works, new fails, old works, new works) was a strong
hint that initialisation was the culprit, but the root cause only became fully clear
when watching the CS line on the oscilloscope: after flashing the new peripheral
firmware, the CS line remained continuously high throughout the startup
initialisation sequence --- confirming that CS was never being asserted.
The fix was to reinitialise SPIM \emph{with} the ss\_pin before calling the Intan
initialisation sequence, then reinitialise it again without the ss\_pin before
handing control to GPIOTE.

\paragraph{Test All Features Regularly.}
Every time a change is made, verify that all existing functionality still works ---
not just the feature under development. There can be non-obvious timing dependencies
between components, and a change to one part of the firmware can silently break
another. Ideally, run a full end-to-end test after every significant change.

\paragraph{Version Control.}
Use \texttt{git} to track all changes. Commit frequently, with a short subject line
and an extended body that explains \emph{why} the change was made, not just what
was changed. A useful commit format in the terminal is:
\begin{lstlisting}[numbers=none,frame=none]
git commit -m "Short summary" \
           -m "Extended explanation: what was tried, what
               failed, and why this approach was chosen."
\end{lstlisting}
Alternatively, maintain a separate plain-text log file alongside the code for
commit annotations. Good version history is invaluable when a previously working
configuration needs to be recovered.

\subsubsection{Potential Issues}
If unexpected channel switching is observed, see the channel resynchronisation
discussion in Section~\ref{sec:gui} (Methods) for the confirmed causes, the
recovery procedure via the \texttt{Resync Channels} button, and the GND-shorting
diagnostic technique.

\paragraph{Noisy or Drifting Signal During Bench Testing.}
When validating recordings on the bench without implanted electrodes, always
short the RHS2116 REF pin to GND. The on-chip amplifiers measure the difference
between each electrode input and the REF pin; without a tissue reference
electrode present, REF floats and acts as an antenna for environmental
interference, which appears as large-amplitude noise on every recorded channel.
Shorting REF to GND provides a stable 0\,V reference and allows clean
function-generator test signals to be observed. On the carrier board connector,
a short wire or solder bridge between the REF and GND pins is sufficient.

\paragraph{Recording Controls.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gui_recording} shows the main recording window.
The channel labels in the plot (Ch01--Ch16) correspond directly to the
Intan RHS2116 pin assignment.

    \centering

    \caption{Main recording window of the Python GUI showing 16-channel live

    \label{fig:gui_recording}

The GUI supports light and dark display themes; click the theme toggle
button in the top-right corner of the main window to switch. The following
steps describe a typical recording session.

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{If recording does not start or restart correctly.}
    Click \textbf{STOP} in the GUI, then power-cycle the peripheral
    system (Intan RHS2116\,+\,nRF52832) completely. After the BLE connection
    re-establishes (LED2 on the central DK lights up), click \textbf{START}.
    This is the most reliable recovery procedure for any unexpected state.

    \item \textbf{Starting a recording.}
    Ensure the J-Link is connected to the central DK. Click \textbf{START};
    the GUI will open a new CSV file in a \texttt{neural\_data/} subdirectory
    next to the script and begin displaying live data. BLE throughput and
    packet loss statistics are shown in the top status bar.

    \item \textbf{Channel switching artefacts.}
    If channel traces appear swapped or offset relative to their expected
    assignments, click \textbf{Resync Channels}. This sends a synchronisation
    command sequence to the peripheral firmware to reinitialise the
    channel-to-data-stream mapping. The recording file is not closed, but
    the peripheral suspends SPI acquisition during the sequence
    (approximately 300\,ms), so samples from that window are absent from
    the data. Recording resumes automatically into the same file once the
    sequence completes.
    See also Section~\ref{sec:gui} in Methods for the confirmed causes of
    channel switching.

    \item \textbf{Stopping a recording.}
    Click \textbf{STOP}. The recording file is closed and the GUI loads the
    recorded data from the CSV for post-recording inspection.

    \item \textbf{Pausing the live plot.}
    Click \textbf{PAUSE PLOT} to freeze the display while recording continues
    in the background. The button label changes to \textbf{RESUME PLOT}.
    Click it again to return to live scrolling.

    \item \textbf{Navigating when the plot is paused.}
    While the plot is paused the status bar reads
    ``\textit{plot paused -- box zoom active}''.
    Mouse interaction changes as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Left mouse drag:} moves the data (pans the view
        left/right and up/down).
        \item \textbf{Right mouse drag:} zooms -- click, hold, and drag
        to scale the view in or out around the cursor position.
    \end{itemize}
    Channel X-axes are also unlinked in this mode, so each channel can be
    navigated independently.

    \item \textbf{Reset Zoom.}
    The \textbf{Reset Zoom} button re-enables auto-range for all channels
    simultaneously, fitting every channel to its full data extent.
    A small reload icon (\raisebox{0pt}{$\circlearrowleft$}) in the
    top-right corner of each individual plot panel resets the zoom for that
    channel only.

    \item \textbf{Sync X (post-recording view).}
    The \textbf{Sync X} toggle button becomes active only after a recording
    is stopped and the data are loaded. When enabled, all channel plots share
    the same horizontal axis, so panning one channel pans all channels
    simultaneously. When disabled, each channel can be scrolled independently.

    \item \textbf{Window size.}
    The \textbf{Window} spin box (row 1, next to PAUSE PLOT) sets the time
    span of the live display in milliseconds. The range is 1--30\,000\,ms;
    the default is 4\,000\,ms (4\,s). Changing this value during a live
    recording takes effect immediately.

    \item \textbf{DS -- display downsampling.}
    The \textbf{DS} spin box (row~1) sets a display-only downsampling factor
    from 1 (no downsampling, default) to~16. Data are always recorded at the
    configured sampling rate regardless of the DS setting; downsampling only
    reduces the number of points rendered per screen update.

    \item \textbf{Plot height.}
    The \textbf{Plot height} slider (row~2) adjusts the vertical height of
    each channel panel between 60 and 600 pixels (default 150\,px). All
    channels resize together.

    \item \textbf{Channel selection, sampling rate, and bandwidth.}
    Row~3 of the control bar provides per-channel checkboxes (Ch01--Ch16),
    a sampling rate spin box, and an upper-bandwidth selector.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Deselecting channels removes them from the display and reduces
        the total BLE throughput requirement, allowing higher per-channel
        rates. The maximum achievable rate for a given channel count is
        limited by the total BLE data rate ($n \times f_\text{ch} \le
        32\,\text{kSa/s}$) and the maximum SPI clock (128\,kHz for
        $n \ge 2$); the GUI enforces these limits automatically and shows
        the current maximum next to the rate selector.
        \item The \textbf{Upper BW} drop-down automatically pre-selects
        the highest Intan RHS2116 upper-bandwidth setting that does not
        exceed the Nyquist frequency ($f_\text{Nyquist} = f_\text{ch}/2$)
        and marks it with ``$\checkmark$ auto''. Bandwidth options above
        the Nyquist frequency are flagged with ``$\triangle$ aliasing'' as
        a warning.
    \end{itemize}

\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Stimulation Controls.}
The stimulation window (Figure~\ref{fig:stim_gui}) is opened by clicking the
\textbf{STIMULATION} button in the main recording window.
\textbf{Stimulation requires an active recording}: clicking
\textbf{START STIM} before recording has been started displays a
``Start recording first!'' warning and does nothing.
When stimulation is active, the live display is suspended and data
continue to accumulate in the background; the display resumes automatically
when stimulation ends.

\subparagraph{Global Parameters} (upper panel, apply equally to all channels):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cat amp / Ano amp}: global default cathodic and anodic
    amplitudes (0--510\,\textmu{}A each, default 100\,\textmu{}A). Changing
    either value propagates to all per-channel rows simultaneously; individual
    channels can be adjusted independently afterwards. The current amplitude
    resolution is fixed at the Intan RHS2116 power-on default of
    2\,\textmu{}A per DAC step, so non-zero values are quantised to
    2, 4, \ldots, 510\,\textmu{}A. Setting the anodic amplitude to
    0\,\textmu{}A disables anodic current output, enabling monophasic
    stimulation (see Section~\ref{sec:gui} for the planned step-size improvement).

    \item \textbf{Cat PW / Ano PW}: cathodic and anodic pulse widths
    (10--10\,000\,\textmu{}s each, default 200\,\textmu{}s). Global-only;
    apply identically to all enabled channels. All pulse widths are quantised
    to multiples of 8\,\textmu{}s by the firmware.

    \item \textbf{Interphase}: delay between the cathodic and anodic phases
    (0--10\,000\,\textmu{}s, default 0\,\textmu{}s).

    \item \textbf{Frequency}: stimulation repetition rate
    (0.1--1000\,Hz, default 400\,Hz).

    \item \textbf{Num pulses} and \textbf{Duration}: total number of biphasic
    pulses and the corresponding stimulation duration. These two fields are
    linked:
    \begin{equation*}
        \text{Duration} = \frac{\text{Num\,pulses}}{\text{Frequency}}
    \end{equation*}
    Changing Num pulses automatically updates Duration, and vice versa.
    Frequency can be changed independently without modifying either.
    Default: 10 pulses, 1\,s at 400\,Hz.
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{Per-Channel Parameters} (lower scrollable panel):
Each of the 16 channel rows contains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Enable checkbox}: if unchecked, the channel is excluded from
    the stimulation run and its waveform preview goes blank.

    \item \textbf{Cat amp / Ano amp}: per-channel cathodic and anodic
    amplitudes, adjustable independently of every other channel and of the
    global defaults above.

    \item \textbf{Waveform preview}: live display of the biphasic pulse for
    this channel, updated on every parameter change.

    \item \textbf{Charge indicator}: charge delivered per phase
    (nC\,=\,amplitude\,\texttimes\,pulse\,width\,/\,1000).
\end{itemize}

The top of the per-channel panel provides additional display controls:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Show checkboxes} (Ch01--Ch16) and \textbf{All} button:
    hide or restore individual channel rows. Hidden channels are still
    stimulated if their enable checkbox is checked.
    \item \textbf{Window}: waveform preview time span (1--10\,000\,ms,
    default 20\,ms).
    \item \textbf{Lock Y-axis}: forces all waveform previews to share the
    same vertical scale, set to the largest amplitude among enabled channels.
\end{itemize}
