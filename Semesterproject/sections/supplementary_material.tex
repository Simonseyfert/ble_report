\subsection{SoftDevice Preemption Timing Derivation}
\label{app:softdevice_timing}

The Nordic S132 SoftDevice preempts the application at every BLE connection 
event to handle radio and stack operations. The timing parameters for a 
peripheral connection event are given in Table~36 of the S132 SoftDevice 
Specification v7.1~\cite{nordic_s132_sds_v71}, reproduced in 
Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing} below. The system does not employ encryption, 
PA/LNA, or the Radio Timeslot API, so typical values apply.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{SoftDevice processor usage during a peripheral connection event 
(reproduced from Nordic S132 SDS v7.1, Table~36~\cite{nordic_s132_sds_v71}).}
\label{tab:softdevice_timing}
\begin{tabular}{llccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Min} & 
\textbf{Typical} & \textbf{Max} \\
\midrule
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioPrepare}}$    & Preparing radio for connection event        & ---          & 51~\textmu s      & 65~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioStart}}$      & Starting the connection event               & ---          & 18~\textmu s      & 24~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}}$ & Processing after sending/receiving a packet & ---          & 60~\textmu s      & 67~\textmu s  \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{PostProcessing}}$  & Processing at end of connection event       & ---          & 90~\textmu s      & 250~\textmu s \\
$t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$                       & Distance between interrupts                 & 183~\textmu s& $>$190~\textmu s  & ---           \\
$t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$                        & Priority level 4 interrupt per packet       & ---          & 40~\textmu s      & ---           \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection*{Single-packet baseline}

The datasheet gives the following formula for one packet sent and received 
per connection event:

For compactness, let $t_\text{RP}$, $t_\text{RS}$, $t_\text{RProc}$, 
$t_\text{PP}$, and $t_4$ denote the timing parameters from 
Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing}. The total preemption time for one 
packet is then:

\begin{equation}
t_{\text{1 pkt}} = t_\text{RP} + t_\text{RS} + t_\text{RProc} + 
t_\text{PP} + 2 \cdot t_4 = 299~\mu\text{s (typical)}
\label{eq:single_packet}
\end{equation}

The factor of $2 \cdot t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ reflects two priority-level-4 interrupts 
fired at the end of the event for stack cleanup and application event delivery, 
both associated with packet reception processing.

\subsection*{Extension to multiple packets per connection event}

At a sampling rate of 32\,000 SPI transactions per second across 16 channels, 
and with 112 samples per BLE packet, the required packet rate is:

\begin{equation}
\frac{32{,}000}{112} \approx 285.7 \text{ packets/s}
\end{equation}

The firmware configures a connection interval range of 7.5--10\,ms; the
empirically negotiated value throughout all experiments in this work was 10\,ms
(confirmed by inspection of the power consumption time course,
Figure~\ref{fig:power_timecourse}). At 10\,ms:

\begin{equation}
285.7 \times 0.010 \approx 2.9 \text{ packets per connection event}
\end{equation}

For completeness, at the minimum configured interval of 7.5\,ms:

\begin{equation}
285.7 \times 0.0075 \approx 2.1 \text{ packets per connection event}
\end{equation}

The system therefore transmits 2--3 packets per connection event; since the
negotiated interval is 10\,ms, the 3-packet scenario is the relevant upper bound. Each 
additional packet beyond the first adds one further 
$t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}}$ interrupt (to handle the 
acknowledgement) and one $t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ interrupt (to deliver the 
\texttt{HVN\_TX\_COMPLETE} event to the application, which triggers 
transmission of the next packet):

\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\text{per extra packet}} = 
t_{\text{ISR}(0),\text{RadioProcessing}} + t_{\text{ISR}(4)} = 
60 + 40 = 100~\text{\textmu s (typical)}
\end{equation}

The total preemption time for 2 and 3 packets per event is therefore:

\begin{align}
t_{\text{2 packets}} &= 299 + 100 = 399~\text{\textmu s} \\
t_{\text{3 packets}} &= 299 + 200 = 499~\text{\textmu s}
\end{align}

This gives a total preemption window of approximately 400--500~\textmu s per
connection event, occurring every 10\,ms (empirically negotiated value;
configured range 7.5--10\,ms).

\subsection*{Meaning of $t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$}

$t_{n\text{ISR}(0)}$ is the minimum time between two consecutive priority-0 ISR
firings within a single connection event. At a minimum of 183\,\textmu{}s, this
is far larger than the DMA buffer swap operation ($\approx$250\,ns), so the swap
is guaranteed to complete before the next SoftDevice interrupt fires.

\subsection*{Worst-case preemption}

Using the maximum column values from Table~\ref{tab:softdevice_timing} (note:
$t_{\text{ISR}(4)}$ has no published maximum; typical value of 40\,\textmu{}s used):
\begin{align}
t_{\text{1 pkt, max}} &= 65 + 24 + 67 + 250 + 2 \times 40 = 486~\text{\textmu{}s} \\
t_{\text{2 pkts, max}} &= 486 + (67 + 40) = 593~\text{\textmu{}s} \\
t_{\text{3 pkts, max}} &= 486 + 2 \times (67 + 40) = 700~\text{\textmu{}s}
\end{align}

At the maximum SPI rate of 128\,kHz (2-channel recording configuration), the time
available to complete the DMA buffer swap before the active buffer overflows is:
\begin{equation}
t_{\text{swap window}} =
(\texttt{DMA\_BUF\_SAMPLES} - 819) \times \frac{1}{128\,\text{kHz}}
= 205 \times 7.8125~\mu\text{s} \approx 1.6~\text{ms}
\end{equation}
The worst-case preemption of 700\,\textmu{}s is safely within the 1.6\,ms window.
At the default configuration (16 channels, 2\,kHz per channel, 32\,kHz SPI), the
window expands to $205 \times 31.25~\mu\text{s} \approx 6.4\,\text{ms}$.

\subsection{Supply Voltage Scaling Analysis}
\label{app:power_voltage}

To quantify the voltage dependence of power consumption, all configurations were
measured at both 3.0\,V and 3.6\,V. Two limiting theoretical models predict
different scaling laws for the ratio $R = P(3.0\,\text{V})\,/\,P(3.6\,\text{V})$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LDO-dominated} ($P \propto V$): excess supply voltage is
    dissipated as heat in a linear regulator, giving
    \begin{equation}
        R_{\text{LDO}} = \frac{3.0}{3.6} \approx 0.833
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic switching-dominated} ($P \propto V^2$, from
    $P_{\text{dyn}} = CV^2f$): CMOS switching losses scale with the square of the
    supply voltage, giving
    \begin{equation}
        R_{\text{CV}^2\!f} = \left(\frac{3.0}{3.6}\right)^{\!2} \approx 0.694
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

The measured ratio across all operating configurations
(Figure~\ref{fig:power_voltage}) has mean $0.765 \pm 0.006$ (std), falling
between the two theoretical references and almost exactly at their midpoint
($0.764$). The low standard deviation ($<1\,\%$) confirms that the voltage
scaling is essentially configuration-independent. An effective exponent $\alpha$
defined by $(3.0/3.6)^\alpha = 0.765$ can be computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \frac{\ln(0.765)}{\ln(3.0/3.6)}
           = \frac{-0.268}{-0.182} \approx 1.47
\end{equation}
This intermediate value ($1 < \alpha < 2$) suggests approximately equal
contributions from linear (LDO-type) dissipation and quadratic (CMOS switching)
losses.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/3V_vs_3_6V.png}
    \caption{Power consumption at 3.6\,V vs 3.0\,V across all measured operating
    configurations. \textit{Top:} absolute power at both voltages vs total BLE
    throughput; grey lines connect matched operating points.
    \textit{Bottom:} measured power ratio $P(3.0\,\text{V})\,/\,P(3.6\,\text{V})$
    per configuration, with dashed reference lines for the LDO model ($P \propto V$,
    ratio $= 0.833$) and CV$^2\!f$ model ($P \propto V^2$, ratio $= 0.694$).
    The measured mean ratio of $0.765 \pm 0.006$ lies between the two references,
    consistent with an effective scaling exponent of $\alpha \approx 1.47$.}
    \label{fig:power_voltage}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Software Requirements: Running the System}
\label{app:software_run}

To run the GUI without modifying firmware, the only prerequisite is the
\textbf{J-Link Software and Documentation Pack} (V8.18 or
later),\footnote{\url{https://www.segger.com/downloads/jlink/}~--- select
\textit{J-Link Software and Documentation Pack}, V8.18.}
which provides the J-Link drivers required by the \texttt{pylink-square}
library for RTT communication with the central DK.

Pre-built executables for Windows, macOS, and Linux are available as
release artefacts on the project
repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Simonseyfert/ble_spi_intan}~---
access upon request; see Section~\ref{sec:gui}.}
and can be launched directly without a Python installation.
To run from source, Python~3.10 or later is required; install dependencies
with \texttt{pip install -r requirements.txt} and launch the main GUI script.

\paragraph{Quick start.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Connect the central nRF52832 DK to the computer via USB and power it on.
    \item Power on the peripheral module (connected to the Intan RHS2116).
    \item Launch the GUI. Once the BLE connection is established LED2 on the
          DK lights up.
    \item Click \textbf{Start Recording}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Code Development}

The following lessons were learned during the development of this project and are
intended to help future developers avoid common pitfalls.

\paragraph{On the use of AI assistants.}
Do not expect to describe the desired functionality or overall architecture to a
large language model and have working embedded firmware in return --- in practice,
nothing will work. Embedded systems programming is fundamentally different from
writing Python scripts: correctness depends on hardware timing, peripheral register
sequences, interrupt priorities, and DMA alignment, none of which an AI can verify
without running the code. That said, AI can still be useful: keeping the
\texttt{main.c} files open in a tool such as Claude Code gives it access to the
current firmware state and allows it to assist with targeted questions. Treat its
output as a starting point to read critically, not as code to paste blindly --- the
closer the change is to the hardware, the more likely it is to contain subtle
errors. Always cross-check generated code against the relevant datasheet.

\paragraph{Step-by-step hardware bring-up.}
New hardware interactions should be developed incrementally, in the smallest
testable steps possible. For example, before implementing the full PPI/DMA
acquisition chain, first verify that the timer can toggle the CS line alone. Add
one PPI channel at a time, confirm each step with the oscilloscope before
proceeding, and only combine components once each is individually validated.
Attempting to implement the full architecture in one step --- especially anything
involving new PPI channels or switching between CPU and hardware control of
peripherals such as the CS line --- will almost always fail and is very difficult
to debug.

\paragraph{Use the oscilloscope.}
Hardware signals should be verified directly with an oscilloscope, not inferred
from software behaviour. Checking the CS line, SCLK, and MISO/MOSI waveforms
at each bring-up step catches problems that would otherwise produce silent failures
or misleading data.

\paragraph{Example.}
When transitioning the CS line from CPU-driven control (via the standard SPI
library) to hardware-driven control via GPIOTE and PPI, the new code appeared to
work correctly on the first test. What was not immediately obvious was why: because
the nRF52832 DK is power-cycled during flashing but VDD on the pin headers remains
active (supplied by the DK's USB regulator), the Intan RHS2116 had never lost
power and still held its register configuration from the previous session. The new
code, which no longer called the Intan initialisation sequence, was simply
inheriting the state left by the old code.

The following morning, after the setup had been powered off overnight, the same
code produced no output at all. Reverting to the old firmware --- which did include
initialisation --- restored operation. Flashing the new code immediately afterward
also worked, because the old firmware had just reinitialised the chip. This
alternating pattern (old works, new fails, old works, new works) was a strong hint
that initialisation was the culprit, but the situation only became fully clear when
watching the CS line on the oscilloscope: after flashing the new peripheral
firmware, the CS line remained continuously high until \textbf{Start Recording}
was pressed --- confirming that the Intan was never being initialised on startup.
The fix was to add the initialisation sequence back into the new code, called once
at boot before the PPI chain is enabled.

\paragraph{Test all features regularly.}
Every time a change is made, verify that all existing functionality still works ---
not just the feature under development. There can be non-obvious timing dependencies
between components, and a change to one part of the firmware can silently break
another. Ideally, run a full end-to-end test after every significant change.

\paragraph{Version control.}
Use \texttt{git} to track all changes. Commit frequently, with a short subject line
and an extended body that explains \emph{why} the change was made, not just what
was changed. A useful commit format in the terminal is:
\begin{lstlisting}[numbers=none,frame=none]
git commit -m "Short summary (50 chars or less)" \
           -m "Extended explanation: what was tried, what
               failed, and why this approach was chosen."
\end{lstlisting}
Alternatively, maintain a separate plain-text log file alongside the code for
commit annotations. Good version history is invaluable when a previously working
configuration needs to be recovered.

\noindent\textbf{Note on VDD and VTG.}
VDD is connected from the DK to the peripheral to power the module (the custom modules
have no USB port). VTG is the J-Link voltage reference input: connecting it to the
peripheral's VDD rail tells the J-Link to match its SWD I/O levels to 3.3\,V,
ensuring reliable communication. Both wires connect to the same VDD rail on the
peripheral.

\noindent\textbf{Important: disconnect the DK after flashing.}
If the DK remains connected, the BLE central may connect to the DK's own nRF52832
target instead of the intended peripheral. Since no Intan chip is connected to the DK,
this results in all-zero recorded signals. The DK must be powered off or its USB
cable disconnected before starting a recording session.
